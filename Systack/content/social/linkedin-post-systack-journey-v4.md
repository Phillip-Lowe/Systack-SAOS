Every training lead learns the same thing eventually: good workers don't quit because the work is hard. They quit because the systems around them are broken.

I saw it for five years in a food distribution warehouse — 50+ people coming through my training, and the ones who stayed weren't the strongest or fastest. They were the ones who didn't have to fight the schedule, hunt down documentation, or ask the same question five different ways to five different people.

I thought leaving that world would mean leaving that problem behind. It didn't.

---

A local deli owner asked me to build an online ordering system. Simple enough — HTML, CSS, JavaScript frontend, n8n handling the backend, zero monthly fees, no platform cut. Saved them the 30% they'd been paying.

Then I watched what actually happened after the order came through.

The owner spent four hours every night answering "can I book Saturday?" for the twelfth time. Checking three apps to see if the order came through. Copying details into a spreadsheet. Sending manual confirmations. Following up with customers who never got their receipt.

The work itself was fine. The admin around it was what drained him.

---

Same pattern, different industry. The problem wasn't the warehouse or the deli. The problem was that small business owners spend their nights on work a script handles in thirty seconds.

So I started building what I wished I'd had in both places.

---

The first piece is Percy — not a chatbot that answers questions, but a harness that actually does things. Structured memory, enforced rules, proactive checks. Something that wakes up knowing what happened yesterday and what needs to happen today.

A customer texts "can I book Saturday?" Percy checks availability, confirms the slot, sends the calendar invite, processes payment, sends the receipt, schedules the follow-up. The owner gets a summary. Zero manual steps.

The second piece is SAOS — the infrastructure I wish I'd had when I was training fifty people with no documentation. Structured data, visual automation, error recovery. Patterns that work whether you're running a warehouse or a deli.

---

The lesson hasn't changed: the people doing the real work shouldn't be drowning in the fake work.
